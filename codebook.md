# Codebook

## 1. Purpose of coding

The coding layer distinguishes preserved source material from interpretive classifications so that sampling discretion and coding discretion remain inspectable.

## 2. Unit of analysis

- Journal policy coding unit: one journal
- Article contribution coding unit: one article
- Evidence unit: one quoted or paraphrased evidential excerpt linked to a coding decision

## 3. Contribution-type definitions

- `local_argumentative`: advances a local thesis, objection, or reply without reordering a broader domain
- `objection_reply`: primarily responds to a specific existing argument or objection
- `interpretive_historical`: centers historical or interpretive reconstruction
- `relational`: clarifies relations among positions, concepts, or debates
- `architectonic`: explicitly organizes or reorganizes a domain, field, theory-space, or problem-space
- `operational_formalizing`: formalizes a framework, model, or procedure
- `applied_problem_focused`: addresses a concrete problem or case domain
- `survey_state_of_field`: maps a literature or field state
- `pedagogical_reconstructive`: clarifies material primarily for reconstruction or teaching
- `other`: anything not captured above, with notes required

## 4. Scope-level definitions

- `local`: narrow claim or exchange
- `subfield`: confined to a recognizable subfield
- `domain`: organizes a broader domain within a subfield or topic area
- `cross_domain`: connects multiple domains or subfields
- `field_wide`: addresses philosophy broadly or a very large field segment
- `unclear`: insufficient basis to classify scope reliably

## 5. Architectonic strength scale

- `0_minimal_argument_structure`: ordinary argumentative ordering only
- `1_local_structural`: some structural organization, but not target architectonic contribution
- `2_domain_architectonic`: explicit organization or reorganization of a domain
- `3_cross_domain_architectonic`: explicit organization or reorganization across domains or field-spaces

For the target study, scores `2_domain_architectonic` and `3_cross_domain_architectonic` count as architectonic contributions.

## 6. Architecture-dependence scale

- `low`: contribution stands without broader organizational framing
- `medium`: broader organization materially supports the contribution
- `high`: contribution depends on explicit reorganization or mapping of a wider domain

## 7. Confidence scale

- `high`: clear textual evidence; classification unlikely to be disputed
- `medium`: plausible classification; at least one alternative is also plausible
- `low`: ambiguous, mixed, or based on insufficient evidence

## 8. Edge cases

Use notes when an article combines argument, interpretation, and organization in a way that makes a single label contestable.

## 9. Mixed cases

Primary contribution type records what the article most directly presents itself as doing. Secondary contribution type records an additional epistemic function. Architecture dependence records whether the primary contribution relies on a wider organizational frame.

## 10. Rules for primary and secondary coding

- Assign exactly one primary contribution type
- Assign secondary contribution type when a second epistemic function is textually supported
- Preserve evidence rows for non-obvious decisions

## 11. Important definition

All theoretical writing is architectonic in a minimal sense because it selects, orders, relates, and stabilizes conceptual materials. This study reserves architectonic contribution for cases in which the primary contribution lies in the explicit organization or reorganization of a domain, cross-domain field, theory-space, or problem-space. Ordinary argumentative structure is not sufficient.

## 12. Limitations

The codebook improves transparency but does not eliminate interpretive judgment. Confidence scores and evidence files are therefore required.
